Concept 1 / 5

Core design ideas: modularity, cohesion, coupling

Break the system into parts (modules). Good parts are cohesive (each does one job) and loosely coupled (changing one doesn't break others).

Example
Keep “reminder logic” separate from “the screen.” Then you can change the UI without touching the reminder rules.
Intro2SE · Ch5
Concept 2 / 5

Architecture = the big boxes

Architecture is the high-level shape: which major parts exist and how they talk. A common simple shape is UI ↔ logic/API ↔ database.

Example
StudyMate: a mobile/web UI, an API/logic layer, a database, plus a scheduler that fires reminders.
Intro2SE · Ch5
Concept 3 / 5

Design decisions & alternatives

A design is a series of choices. Record the alternatives you considered and WHY you picked one — that's engineering, not guessing.

Example
Reminder check: option A = check when the app opens; option B = a daily scheduled job. Chose B because reminders must fire even if the app is closed.
Intro2SE · Ch5
Concept 4 / 5

UI basics: wireframes

A wireframe is a rough sketch of a screen (boxes & labels), drawn before coding. Cheap to change, fast to get feedback. (Deep UX is a separate course.)

Example
Sketch the StudyMate “week view”: 7 columns, tasks as cards, a + button to add.
Intro2SE · Ch5
Concept 5 / 5

Design must trace to requirements

Every design element should satisfy some requirement. If it doesn't, it's probably scope creep.

Example
The scheduler exists to satisfy FR-3 (reminders) + NFR-2 (fires within 1 minute). That's its reason to exist.
