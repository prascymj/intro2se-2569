Concept 1 / 5

What a requirement is (FR vs NFR)

A requirement states what the system must do or how well it must do it. Functional (FR) = a feature/behavior. Non-functional (NFR) = a quality constraint like performance, security, or privacy.

Example
FR: “the app reminds me before a deadline.” NFR: “a reminder appears within 1 minute,” “a student sees only their own tasks.”

Concept 2 / 5

Elicitation — finding the real need

You discover requirements by talking to and observing users, not by guessing. Ask about goals and pain, not solutions.

Example
Don't ask “do you want a red button?” Ask “tell me about the last time you missed a deadline.” The need hides in the story.

Concept 3 / 5

User stories

A simple format that keeps the user's goal central: “As a <user>, I want <goal>, so that <benefit>.”

Example
“As a student, I want a reminder 1 day before a deadline, so that I don't submit late.”
Intro2SE · Ch3
Concept 4 / 5

Acceptance criteria

Concrete conditions that decide whether a story is “done.” They later become your tests. Write them as checkable statements.

Example
For the reminder story: (1) 1 day before an open task → reminder appears; (2) a completed task → no reminder.
Intro2SE · Ch3
Concept 5 / 5

The SRS and scope

The SRS collects the requirements in one place. Just as important: state what is OUT of scope, so the team doesn't drift.

Example
StudyMate SRS in scope: tasks, reminders, week view. Out of scope: chat, payments, AI tutor (for now).
